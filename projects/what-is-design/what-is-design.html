What's Design?

Essential Principles

Chance Castaneda
June 15
Braun

We should surround ourselves with fewer, but better things. This is not a constraint, but an advantage, which allows more space for real life.

Braun T 580. Designed by Dieter Rams, 1961.

Deference

The most important question in design is: what lasts and why? When you look at Braun’s enormous range of well-designed products, you’ll find that a majority of them have stood the test of time. That’s because its Bauhaus aesthetic humbly submits to and respects one’s visual and physical environment: enclosures feature neutral tones, with saturated hues creating counterpoints that indicate function; handles make products easy to touch and move; and proportional forms make products easy to scan.

Throughout Braun’s work, there is a persistent respect for customer self-expression and pre-existing behavioral systems, imposing orientation only as much as it builds clarity between people and their tools. But deference for people is not inevitable. It is an untiring effort, never more than one design away from eradication.

Braun KM 3. Designed by Gerd Alfred Muller, 1957.

Clarity

Braun’s products are consistent, coherent, and easy to learn, which makes an honest relationship with people possible. Material, color, form, and proportion are reliable indicators of function, simplifying complex systems into essential, parseable components of a larger whole.

Clarity is tied directly to perceived agency: the more you understand how something works, the more you can negotiate with it. Knowing the existing components of a mixer gives you ideas on how to improve them, to know what is missing and what should be there.

Braun RCS 9 / L20. Designed by Dieter Rams, 1961-1962.

Fluency

Braun’s products speak the same language. In their hi-fi audio department, products are designed to work together, enabling smooth and natural interaction across tools.

A curated palette of neutrals and color counterpoints, well-defined proportions that build ecosystem cohesion, and high-contrast controls that communicates state from a distance all reinforce fluency.

Apple

Truly believe. That by making something useful, empowering and beautiful, you express your love for humanity.

Apple Watch. Designed by Apple, 2014.

Simplicity

Its utility is purely psychological. But once you experience simplicity and get a sense that everything could share this clarity, this consistency of care, you can never go back.

If you held up any of their breakthrough products — Mac, iPod, iPhone, iPad, AirPods — it would be easy to see why Apple exists on Earth. Apple’s ability to parallel design and engineer many aspects of an experience, from software and hardware to marketing and services, uniquely positions them to nurture, apply, and execute imagination in the most creative ways.

Maps, iOS 17 / watchOS 10. Designed by Apple, 2023.

Continuity

Flow is an important part of a delightful experience. It’s the melding of thought and action: whatever you think to do, you do it effortlessly. That means the next thing you need is always nearby and easy to get to.

Apple’s services, from Maps to Fitness, feature enough personality to make them instantly recognizable across devices, reducing friction. Consistency across typography, color, proportion, motion, flow, and interaction techniques and input support continuity.

TV in visionOS. Designed by Apple, 2023.

Care

In spite of numerous constraints and aims for consistency, designs ought to exemplify picking the right tool for the job. It takes expert discernment with the utmost clarity, integrity, and care to make the right call for the right reasons.

Ownership of the experience grants one the responsibility to go beyond the obvious alternatives and ship experiences that speak to the best of the arts and sciences. It’s that care for our humanity that forces Apple to consider scenarios where your content reflects in the ripples of pond water or where notifications tap your wrist with organic friendliness.
